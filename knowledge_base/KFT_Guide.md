Kidney Function Test (KFT)
Overview

Kidney Function Tests (KFTs) assess how effectively the kidneys:

Filter waste products from blood
Maintain fluid balance
Regulate electrolytes
Control acid-base balance
Support blood pressure regulation

KFTs help detect:

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)
Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
Dehydration
Electrolyte imbalances
Urinary tract obstruction
Medication-related kidney damage
KEY KFT PARAMETERS
Serum Creatinine
What it Measures

Creatinine is a waste product generated from normal muscle metabolism.

Healthy kidneys remove creatinine from the bloodstream.

Clinical Importance

Creatinine is one of the most important markers of kidney filtration function.

High Creatinine May Indicate
Acute kidney injury
Chronic kidney disease
Urinary obstruction
Severe dehydration
Medication-induced kidney damage
Low Creatinine May Indicate

Usually not concerning.

May occur with:

Low muscle mass
Malnutrition
Pregnancy
Interpretation Notes

Creatinine should never be interpreted alone.

Always evaluate with:

eGFR
BUN/Urea
Age
Sex
Hydration status
Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) / Urea
What it Measures

Urea is a waste product formed during protein metabolism.

It is filtered by the kidneys.

High Urea/BUN

Possible causes:

Kidney dysfunction
Dehydration
High protein intake
Gastrointestinal bleeding
Heart failure
Low Urea/BUN

Possible causes:

Liver disease
Malnutrition
Low protein intake
Excess fluid intake
Clinical Importance

Often rises before creatinine in dehydration.

BUN-to-Creatinine Ratio
Formula

BUN ÷ Creatinine

Interpretation
Elevated Ratio (>20:1)

May suggest:

Dehydration
Reduced kidney blood flow
Gastrointestinal bleeding
Normal Ratio

May suggest intrinsic kidney disease if both values are elevated.

Low Ratio

May occur in:

Liver disease
Severe malnutrition
ESTIMATED GLOMERULAR FILTRATION RATE (eGFR)
eGFR
What it Measures

Estimated amount of blood filtered by kidneys per minute.

Clinical Importance

Most useful indicator of overall kidney function.

Interpretation
Normal

≥90 mL/min/1.73m²

Mild Reduction

60–89

Moderate Reduction

30–59

Severe Reduction

15–29

Kidney Failure

<15

Important Note

A single low eGFR does not automatically indicate CKD.

Persistent reduction for more than 3 months is generally required.

ELECTROLYTES

Kidneys regulate electrolyte balance.

Sodium (Na⁺)
Function

Maintains:

Fluid balance
Blood pressure
Nerve function
High Sodium (Hypernatremia)

Possible causes:

Dehydration
Excess salt intake
Diabetes insipidus
Low Sodium (Hyponatremia)

Possible causes:

Heart failure
Kidney disease
Excess fluid intake
SIADH
Symptoms of Severe Abnormality
Confusion
Seizures
Altered consciousness
Potassium (K⁺)
Function

Essential for:

Heart rhythm
Muscle function
Nerve conduction
High Potassium (Hyperkalemia)

Possible causes:

Kidney failure
Certain medications
Tissue breakdown
Risks

Can cause:

Dangerous arrhythmias
Cardiac arrest
Low Potassium (Hypokalemia)

Possible causes:

Vomiting
Diarrhea
Diuretics
Symptoms
Muscle weakness
Heart rhythm disturbances
Chloride (Cl⁻)
Function

Maintains:

Acid-base balance
Fluid regulation
High Chloride

May occur in:

Dehydration
Metabolic acidosis
Low Chloride

May occur in:

Vomiting
Metabolic alkalosis
Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)
Function

Represents body's acid-base buffering system.

Low Bicarbonate

Suggests:

Metabolic acidosis
Advanced kidney disease
High Bicarbonate

Suggests:

Metabolic alkalosis
Excess vomiting
URIC ACID
What it Measures

Waste product from purine metabolism.

High Uric Acid

May occur in:

Gout
Kidney disease
High-purine diet
Tumor lysis syndrome
Low Uric Acid

Usually less clinically significant.

Calcium
High Calcium

Possible causes:

Hyperparathyroidism
Certain cancers
Excess vitamin D
Low Calcium

Possible causes:

Chronic kidney disease
Vitamin D deficiency
Phosphorus
High Phosphorus

Common in:

Chronic kidney disease
Low Phosphorus

May occur in:

Malnutrition
Vitamin D deficiency
IMPORTANT KFT INTERPRETATION PATTERNS
Dehydration

Typical Pattern

Urea ↑
BUN/Creatinine Ratio ↑
Sodium ↑
Creatinine mildly ↑

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

Typical Pattern

Creatinine ↑ rapidly
eGFR ↓
Potassium may ↑

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Typical Pattern

Creatinine ↑
eGFR ↓ persistently
Phosphorus ↑
Calcium ↓
Hemoglobin ↓ (if CBC available)

Hyperkalemia Risk Pattern

Typical Pattern

Potassium ↑
Creatinine ↑
eGFR ↓

Gout Pattern

Typical Pattern

Uric Acid ↑
Kidney function normal or mildly impaired
